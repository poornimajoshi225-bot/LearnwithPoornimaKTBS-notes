10th Class English First Language - Part 1

Poem 4
Lochinvar

(For Memorization - First Four Stanzas Only)

— Sir Walter Scott

Poem

O, young Lochinvar is come out of the west,

Through all the wide Border his steed was the best;

And save his good broadsword he weapons had none,

He rode all unarm’d and he rode all alone.

So faithful in love, and so dauntless in war,

There never was knight like the young Lochinvar.

He staid not for brake, and he stopp’d not for stone,

He swam the Eske river where ford there was none;

But ere he alighted at Netherby gate,

The bride had consented, the gallant came late:

For a laggard in love, and a dastard in war,

Was to wed the fair Ellen of brave Lochinvar.

So boldly he enter’d the Netherby Hall,

Among bride’s-men, and kinsmen, and brothers and all:

Then spoke the bride’s father, his hand on his sword

(For the poor craven bridegroom said never a word).

“O come ye in peace here, or come ye in war,

Or to dance at our bridal, young Lord Lochinvar?”

“I long woo’d your daughter, my suit you denied;

Love swells like the Solway, but ebbs like its tide—

And now am I come with this lost love of mine,

To lead but one measure, drink one cup of wine.

There are maidens in Scotland more lovely by far,

That would gladly be bride to the young Lochinvar.”

The bride kiss’d the goblet: the knight took it up,

He quaff’d off the wine, and he threw down the cup.

She look’d down to blush, and she look’d up to sigh,

With a smile on her lips, and a tear in her eye.

He took her soft hand, ere her mother could bar,

“Now tread we a measure!” said young Lochinvar.

So stately his form, and so lovely her face,

That never a hall such a galliard did grace;

While her mother did fret, and her father did fume,

And the bridegroom stood dangling his bonnet and plume;

And the bride-maidens whisper’d, “’Twere better by far,

To have match’d our fair cousin with young Lochinvar.”

One touch to her hand, and one word in her ear,

When they reach’d the hall-door, and the charger stood near;

So light to the croupe the fair lady he swung,

So light to the saddle before her he sprung!

“She is won! We are gone, over bank, bush, and scaur;

They’ll have fleet steeds that follow,” quoth young Lochinvar.

There was mounting ’mong Graemes of the Netherby clan;

Forsters, Fenwicks, and Musgraves, they rode and they ran:

There was racing and chasing on Cannobie Lee,

But the lost bride of Netherby ne’er did they see.

So daring in love, and so dauntless in war,

Have ye e’er heard of gallant like young Lochinvar?

II. COMPREHENSION QUESTIONS

A. Answer briefly the following questions.

1. Who was Lochinvar? Why did he ride to Netherby Hall?

Answer:

Lochinvar was a brave and gallant young knight from the West. He rode to Netherby Hall to stop the wedding of his beloved, Ellen, who was being forced to marry another man.

2. What qualities of Lochinvar are highlighted in the first stanza?

Answer:
  • Faithful in love
  • Dauntless in war
  • Fearless
  • Self-reliant
  • Courageous
  • Rode alone with only his broadsword

3. Lochinvar rode the horse passionately to meet Ellen. What actions in lines 7 and 8 reveal his hurried movements?

Answer:

The lines "He staid not for brake, and he stopp'd not for stone. He swam the Eske river where ford there was none." show that Lochinvar never stopped for any obstacle. He even swam across the river without a bridge because he wanted to reach Ellen before the wedding.

4. What qualities of the bridegroom are listed in stanza 2?

Answer:
  • Laggard in love (slow and unenthusiastic)
  • Dastard in war (cowardly)
  • Weak
  • Timid

5. What evidence is there in stanza 3 to show that the bridegroom was a coward?

Answer:

The poem says, "For the poor craven bridegroom said never a word." Although Lochinvar boldly entered the hall, the bridegroom remained silent and showed no courage.

6. How did the bride's father receive Lochinvar? And what did he ask him?

Answer:

Ellen's father received Lochinvar cautiously with his hand on his sword. He asked whether Lochinvar had come in peace, in war, or simply to dance at the wedding.

7. What answer did Lochinvar give the bride's father?

Answer:

Lochinvar replied that he had long loved Ellen, but his proposal had been rejected. He said he had come only to dance one measure with his lost love and drink one cup of wine before leaving.

8. What quality of love is highlighted by Lochinvar when he says,

a. "Love swells like the Solway, but ebbs like its tide."

This comparison suggests that love can become strong and passionate, yet may also appear to fade away like the changing tides of the River Solway.

b. Does this truly reflect Lochinvar's love for Ellen?

No. Lochinvar's love for Ellen is deep, true and unwavering. He never stopped loving her.

c. If not, why does Lochinvar tell him what is not true?

Lochinvar says this to deceive Ellen's father. He wants everyone to believe that he has accepted defeat so that they will not suspect his real plan of taking Ellen away.

9. What other lies does Lochinvar tell Ellen's father?

Answer:

Lochinvar falsely says that there are many maidens in Scotland who are more beautiful than Ellen and that they would gladly marry him. He says this only to hide his true intention of eloping with Ellen.

10. How did Ellen express her love towards Lochinvar?

Answer:
  • She kissed the goblet after Lochinvar drank from it.
  • She smiled lovingly at him.
  • A tear appeared in her eye.
  • She willingly held his hand.
  • She danced happily with him.
  • She trusted him and escaped with him.

11. What was the impression of the bride-maidens about the pair—Lochinvar and Ellen?

Answer:

The bride-maidens felt that Ellen and Lochinvar were the perfect pair. They whispered that it would have been far better if fair Ellen had married the brave and gallant Lochinvar instead of the cowardly bridegroom.

12. What mixed feelings are brought out in stanza 5?

Answer:
  • Lochinvar and Ellen were happy as they danced together.
  • Ellen smiled with joy but also shed a tear.
  • Her mother was worried and angry.
  • Her father was furious.
  • The bridegroom stood silently without courage.

Thus, the stanza presents joy, love, fear, anger and disappointment at the same time.

13. What clues of his plan does Lochinvar give Ellen?

Answer:

Lochinvar secretly touched Ellen's hand and whispered a few words into her ear. He led her towards the hall door where his horse was waiting. These actions revealed his plan to escape with her.

14. Answer the following.

a. What brings a smile on Ellen's face?

Ellen smiles because she is overjoyed to see Lochinvar, the man she truly loves, and realizes that he has come to rescue her.

b. Why does she shed a tear?

She sheds a tear because she is emotional about leaving her family, the danger of their escape, and the uncertainty of the future.

15. Pick out all the adjectives that describe Lochinvar.

Answer:
Young Faithful Dauntless Brave Bold Gallant Daring Stately Fearless Loyal Courageous

B. CLOSE STUDY

Read the following extracts carefully. Discuss in pairs and then answer the questions given below them.

1. "Love swells like the Solway but ebbs like its tide."

a. What is Solway?

The Solway is the Solway Firth, an inlet of the sea that separates part of Scotland from England. It is famous for its powerful and rapidly changing tides.

b. Who does Lochinvar speak this line to?

Lochinvar speaks this line to the bride's father at Netherby Hall.

c. What is the figure of speech used here?

The figure of speech used is Simile.

d. Explain the figure of speech.

A Simile is a comparison using the words "like" or "as". Here, Lochinvar compares love to the changing tides of the Solway Firth to suggest that love rises and falls like the tides. However, this is only a clever trick to make Ellen's father believe that his love has faded.

2. "Then spoke the bride's father, his hand on his sword
(For the poor craven bridegroom, said never a word)."

a. What did the bride's father speak?

He asked Lochinvar whether he had come in peace, in war, or simply to dance at the wedding.

b. Why did he keep his hand on his sword?

He kept his hand on his sword because he was suspicious of Lochinvar's sudden arrival and was prepared to fight if necessary.

c. Why didn't the bridegroom say even a word?

The bridegroom was a coward. He was frightened by Lochinvar's bravery and lacked the courage to speak or challenge him.

III. PARAGRAPH WRITING

Discuss in pairs/groups of four each the answers to the following questions. Note down the important points for each question and then develop the points into one-paragraph answers.

1. Pick out all the details from the poem which help Lochinvar to hide his real intentions.

Key Points

  • Feigned detachment: He claims that there are other maidens in Scotland "more lovely by far."
  • The Solway comparison: He says his love has faded like the ebbing tide.
  • Limited request: He asks only for one dance and one cup of wine.
  • Calm behaviour: He remains polite and composed so that no one suspects his real plan.

Paragraph Answer

Lochinvar cleverly hides his real intentions through his intelligence and presence of mind. He tells Ellen's father that his love has faded like the tides of the Solway and even claims that there are many beautiful maidens in Scotland who would happily marry him. He politely asks only for one dance and one cup of wine, making everyone believe that he has accepted defeat. His calm and respectful behaviour prevents anyone from suspecting his true plan. As a result, he gets the perfect opportunity to take Ellen away safely.

2. How did Lochinvar finally win Ellen for himself?

Key Points

  • Bold entry: He entered Netherby Hall fearlessly.
  • Won Ellen's confidence: During the dance he whispered his escape plan to her.
  • Quick action: Before anyone could stop them, he led Ellen to the waiting horse.
  • Swift escape: He lifted Ellen onto his horse and rode away immediately.
  • The pursuers failed: The clans chased them but could not catch the brave knight.

Paragraph Answer

Lochinvar won Ellen through his courage, intelligence and quick action. He entered Netherby Hall boldly and behaved politely so that no one suspected his plan. While dancing with Ellen, he secretly whispered his plan of escape. At the right moment, he quickly led her to the waiting horse, lifted her onto the saddle and galloped away before anyone could stop them. Although many members of the Netherby clan chased them, they failed to catch the brave and fearless Lochinvar. Thus, his courage and presence of mind helped him win Ellen.

3. "Only the brave deserve the fair." Justify the statement with reference to the poem.

Key Points

  • Contrast of characters: Lochinvar is brave and fearless, whereas the bridegroom is timid and cowardly.
  • Risk-taking: Lochinvar enters the enemy's hall alone to rescue Ellen.
  • True love: He risks his life for the woman he truly loves.
  • The bridegroom's weakness: He remains silent and does nothing.
  • Outcome: Lochinvar's courage earns him Ellen's love and respect.

Paragraph Answer

Sir Walter Scott's poem Lochinvar beautifully proves the saying, "Only the brave deserve the fair." Lochinvar is a fearless knight who risks his life to rescue the woman he truly loves. He enters Netherby Hall alone, cleverly hides his intentions and boldly carries Ellen away before anyone can stop him. On the other hand, the bridegroom is described as a coward who remains silent throughout the incident. Ellen finally goes with the brave and gallant Lochinvar because courage, true love and determination make him worthy of winning her. Thus, the poem clearly justifies that only a brave person deserves true love.
