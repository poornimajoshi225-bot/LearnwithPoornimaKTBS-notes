10th Standard English First Language - Part 1

Lesson 4

Louis Pasteur, Conqueror of Disease

— E. H. Carter

Lesson Overview

This lesson explains the inspiring life of the great French scientist Louis Pasteur, who dedicated his life to fighting deadly diseases. His discoveries in microbiology, vaccination, and pasteurization saved millions of lives and transformed modern medicine.

II. COMPREHENSION QUESTIONS

A. Answer the following questions briefly.

1. "Louis Pasteur was not a soldier, but he was a fighter." What does the statement mean?
This means that although Pasteur never fought in wars, he devoted his entire life to fighting diseases. He courageously battled illness through scientific research and worked tirelessly to save millions of lives.
2. What are bacteria?
Bacteria are tiny rod-shaped living organisms found in air, water, soil, and the bodies of living things. Some cause diseases while others are useful to nature.
3. As a scientist, how was Louis Pasteur unique?
Louis Pasteur combined scientific research with practical applications. His discoveries helped both industries and humanity by preventing diseases.
4. How did Pasteur try to solve difficult problems?
He would sit quietly for hours, thinking deeply until he found the correct solution.
5(a). How would Pasteur react whenever he solved a problem?
His face would light up with joy, and he would eagerly share the discovery with his wife and assistants.
5(b). What does this reveal about him as a person?
It shows that he was humble, enthusiastic, hardworking, and loved sharing success with others.
6. What discoveries did Pasteur make about yeast?
He proved that yeast is made of tiny living cells and that healthy yeast is essential for proper fermentation.
7. What does "spontaneous generation" mean?
It is the old belief that germs arise by themselves without any parent germs.
8. Did Pasteur believe in spontaneous generation? If not, what did he believe?
No. He believed that germs come from other germs and are carried through the air.
9. What did Pasteur prove?
He proved that germs are carried through dust in the air and do not appear spontaneously.
10. Which soup was the worst, better and best in the second soup experiment?
  • Worst: Soup kept in the hotel bedroom.
  • Better: Soup kept in the field.
  • Best: Soup kept on the mountain top.
11. What is Pasteurisation?
Pasteurisation is the process of heating milk or other liquids to about 50–60°C to destroy harmful germs without affecting quality.
12. What quality of Pasteur is highlighted?
His love for humanity and his desire to use science only for the welfare of mankind.
13. On what study did Pasteur spend three years?
He spent three years studying the disease affecting silkworms.
14. How is vaccination different from inoculation?
Vaccination protects against disease using vaccines, whereas inoculation introduces weakened germs to develop immunity.
15. What made Pasteur believe that a cow could not have anthrax twice?
He noticed that cows which recovered from anthrax did not get the disease again.
16. What made many scientists angry?
His proposal to inject weakened germs into healthy animals and humans made many scientists angry.
17. What is rabies?
Rabies is a deadly disease spread through the bite of an infected dog.
18. How was Pasteur a better scientist than Jenner?
Pasteur understood the role of germs and developed vaccines for several diseases using scientific methods.
19. How was the low death rate during World War I a tribute to Pasteur?
Soldiers were protected through vaccination based on Pasteur's discoveries, greatly reducing deaths from infectious diseases.
20(a). Which are the two opposing laws?
The law of blood and death, and the law of peace, work and health.
20(b). Which law did Pasteur favour?
He supported the law of peace, work and health.
21. What was Pasteur's message to young students?
Study hard and work for the progress and welfare of your country and humanity.
22. What is the supreme happiness?
The supreme happiness is knowing that one has contributed to the progress and welfare of humanity.
23. Explain Pasteur's conclusions.
  • a) Yeast consists of tiny living cells.
  • b) Germs are carried through the air and spread by contact.
  • c) Fresh mountain air contains fewer germs than stale air.
  • d) Heating milk or wine kills harmful germs and makes them safe.

B. CLOSE STUDY

Read the following extracts carefully. Discuss in pairs and then write the answers to the questions given below them.

Extract 1

“Do germs form from other germs, or do they just come of themselves?”
a) What did people like Pasteur actually believe about germs?
Pasteur believed that germs do not appear by themselves. They are carried through the air and spread by coming into contact with other objects.
b) Which of the two was called "spontaneous generation"?
The belief that germs arise on their own without parent germs was called spontaneous generation.
c) How did Pasteur prove that he was right?
Pasteur boiled soup in special swan-neck bottles to destroy existing germs. The curved neck trapped dust and germs, keeping the soup fresh. When the soup touched the trapped dust, it spoiled. This proved that germs are carried through the air and do not arise spontaneously.

Extract 2

“If you can cure animals, you can cure my son.”
a) What had Pasteur cured the animals of?
Pasteur successfully cured animals suffering from the deadly disease called anthrax.
b) What was wrong with the "son" referred to?
The boy had been bitten by a rabid (mad) dog and was suffering from the deadly disease called rabies.
c) Did Pasteur cure the boy? How?
Yes. Pasteur treated the boy by injecting weakened rabies germs (vaccination). The treatment was successful, and the boy recovered completely.

III. PARAGRAPH WRITING

Discuss in pairs/groups of four each, the answers to the following questions. Individually, note down the important points for each question and then develop them into one-paragraph answers.

1. What was troubling the French wine-growers? How did Pasteur solve their problem? What is the name of the process?

French wine-growers faced a serious problem because harmful germs were causing their wine to turn sour, resulting in heavy losses. Louis Pasteur carefully studied the problem and discovered that these germs could be destroyed by heating the wine to about 50–60°C without affecting its quality. This process prevented spoilage and saved the wine industry. The process became famous as Pasteurisation, which is still used today to make milk and other liquids safe by destroying harmful bacteria.

2. How did Pasteur develop the idea of immunity?

Louis Pasteur developed the idea of immunity while studying the deadly disease anthrax. He observed that animals which recovered from anthrax never suffered from it again. This made him believe that a weakened form of the germ could protect healthy animals from future infection. He proved his theory through successful experiments, laying the foundation for modern vaccination and immunization.

3. What human qualities of Pasteur do you admire?

Louis Pasteur was hardworking, humble, patient, kind, and completely dedicated to humanity. He spent long hours thinking deeply about difficult problems and never gave up until he found solutions. He used his scientific knowledge only to help people and never for destructive purposes. His love for mankind, perseverance, and selfless service make him one of the greatest scientists in history.

4. "All the research which he did in his laboratories was meant to help his fellow human beings." Elucidate.

Every discovery made by Louis Pasteur was intended to improve human life. He solved problems faced by wine-growers, silk farmers, and livestock owners. He discovered pasteurisation, developed vaccines against deadly diseases such as anthrax and rabies, and saved millions of lives. Pasteur believed that science should always serve humanity. His entire life was devoted to reducing suffering and improving the welfare of people.

5. What would have been the fate of humankind if Louis Pasteur had not discovered the vaccine against rabies?

Without Louis Pasteur's discovery of the rabies vaccine, countless people would have continued to die from this deadly disease. At that time, a bite from a rabid dog was almost always fatal. Pasteur's vaccine saved thousands of lives and opened the way for the development of many modern vaccines. His contribution has protected humanity from dangerous infectious diseases and greatly improved public health throughout the world.

IV. VOCABULARY EXERCISES

B. Discuss in pairs the difference between the words in each pair. Frame sentences to bring out the difference.

1. Bravery   –   Boldness

Bravery Boldness
Courage in facing danger or fear. Confidence in taking risks or acting fearlessly.

Sentence (Bravery): The soldier showed great bravery in saving innocent people.

Sentence (Boldness): His boldness impressed everyone during the debate.

2. Quite – Quiet

Quite Quiet
Means completely or fairly. Means silent or without noise.

Sentence (Quite): The movie was quite interesting.

Sentence (Quiet): Please keep quiet during the examination.

3. Infectious – Contagious

Infectious Contagious
Spread by germs through air, water or objects. Spread mainly through direct contact.

Sentence (Infectious): Tuberculosis is an infectious disease.

Sentence (Contagious): Chickenpox is a contagious disease.

4. Clever – Intelligent

Clever Intelligent
Quick in solving problems. Having high mental ability.

Sentence (Clever): Riya found a clever solution to the puzzle.

Sentence (Intelligent): She is an intelligent student.

5. Further – Farther

Further Farther
Means additional or more. Means physical distance.

Sentence (Further): Further information will be given tomorrow.

Sentence (Farther): We walked farther than yesterday.

6. Discovery – Invention

Discovery Invention
Finding something already existing. Creating something new.

Sentence (Discovery): Pasteur's discovery saved many lives.

Sentence (Invention): The invention of the telephone changed communication.

7. Vaccination – Inoculation

Vaccination Inoculation
Giving a vaccine to prevent disease. Introducing weakened germs to build immunity.

Sentence (Vaccination): Every child should receive vaccination.

Sentence (Inoculation): Pasteur's inoculation experiments were successful.

8. Due to – Owing to

Due to Owing to
Means caused by. Means because of.

Sentence (Due to): The match was cancelled due to rain.

Sentence (Owing to): Owing to heavy traffic, we reached late.

9. Little – A little

Little A little
Almost no amount. A small but sufficient amount.

Sentence (Little): There is little water in the bottle.

Sentence (A little): I need a little sugar for my tea.

10. Between – Among

Between Among
Used for two persons or things. Used for three or more persons or things.

Sentence (Between): Divide the chocolates between the two children.

Sentence (Among): The teacher distributed prizes among the students.

B. WORD STRESS

Word Stress means giving extra force or emphasis to one syllable of a word while pronouncing it.

What is a Syllable?

A syllable is a unit of pronunciation that contains one vowel sound. Words may have one or more syllables. In words having more than one syllable, one syllable is stressed more than the others.
