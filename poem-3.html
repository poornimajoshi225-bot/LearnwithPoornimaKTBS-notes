10th Class English First Language Part 1

Poem 3
The Temple and the Body (Vachana)

(For Memorisation)

Poet : Basavanna
Translator : A.K. Ramanujam

The Poem

The rich
Will make temples for Shiva.

What shall I,
A poor man,
Do?

My legs are pillars,
The body the shrine,
The head a cupola
Of gold.

Listen, O Lord of the Meeting Rivers,
Things standing shall fall,
But the moving ever shall stay.

About the Poet

Basavanna was a great philosopher, poet, social reformer and saint of Karnataka. He believed that true devotion is more important than expensive rituals and temples. His Vachanas are short spiritual poems that teach equality, honesty and simple living.

Summary

In this Vachana, Basavanna says that wealthy people may build beautiful temples for Lord Shiva, but poor people cannot afford them. Instead, he considers his own body to be God's temple. His legs are the pillars, his body is the shrine and his head is the golden dome. He concludes that buildings made of stone may one day collapse, but a life filled with devotion and good deeds will remain forever. The poem teaches that true worship comes from purity of heart rather than costly temples.

Central Idea

God does not live only inside temples. A pure heart, good character and sincere devotion make every human body a living temple.

Moral

  • True devotion is greater than wealth.
  • God values honesty and goodness.
  • Our body should be kept pure through good thoughts and actions.
  • Simple living and sincere faith are the greatest forms of worship.

II. COMPREHENSION QUESTIONS

A. Answer briefly the following questions:

1. The lines “The rich / will make temples for Siva,” implies that

Answer: b. they can afford to build temples.

2. Which of the following statements are true?

Answer:

  • b. It’s not possible for the speaker to build a temple.
  • d. The speaker believes that God is present within ourselves and not in any temple.

3. What are the feelings of the speaker, suggested in the question, “What shall I, a poor man, do?”

Answer:

  • a. Helplessness
  • f. Anguish

Explanation: The speaker feels excluded from traditional religious practices because of poverty, yet expresses sincere devotion and humility.

4. The word “Listen” in the last stanza is

Answer: b. an appeal.

5. The expression “Things Standing,” suggests

Answer:

  • a. Any man-made temple.
  • c. Anything which is static.

6. There are two examples of paradox in the last two lines.

i) What opposite ideas are suggested in “Things standing shall fall”?

Man-made temples appear permanent, yet they are temporary and will eventually collapse. The paradox contrasts permanence with destruction.

ii) What opposite ideas are suggested in “The moving shall ever stay”?

The human body is always moving and changing, yet true devotion and the living spirit remain forever. The paradox contrasts change with eternity.

7. What final message do the last two lines convey?

Basavanna teaches that true spirituality comes from within. Stone temples may crumble with time, but sincere devotion, good deeds and a pure heart remain forever. God lives in every devoted human being rather than only in man-made temples.

B. CLOSE STUDY

Read the following extract carefully. Discuss in pairs and then write the answers to the questions given below it.

My legs are pillars,
the body the shrine,
the head a cupola of gold.

1. How is the human body compared to a temple?

The speaker uses architectural imagery to compare the human body to a temple.

  • Legs are compared to the pillars that support a temple.
  • Body is compared to the shrine, the sacred place where God resides.
  • Head is compared to a golden cupola (dome), the highest and most beautiful part of a temple.

Through these comparisons, Basavanna presents the human body as a sacred temple where God lives.

2. What is the figure of speech used here?

The main figure of speech is Metaphor, because the poet directly compares the body to a temple without using the words "like" or "as".

The stanza also contains an Extended Metaphor because the comparison continues throughout the entire passage by comparing different parts of the body with different parts of a temple.

3. Why is the speaker comparing the human body to a temple?

Basavanna compares the human body to a temple to teach that God lives within every human being and not only inside expensive temples.

  • True worship comes from a pure heart.
  • Every person, rich or poor, can worship God.
  • There is no need for wealth to build a temple because the body itself is God's temple.
  • The poem emphasizes equality, devotion and inner spirituality.

III. PARAGRAPH WRITING

Discuss in pairs/groups of four each and answer the following question. Note down the important points and then develop the points into a paragraph.

1. What ideas of spirituality emerge from the study of this well-known Vachana?

Key Ideas of Spirituality

  • Spirituality is open to everyone, irrespective of wealth or social status.
  • God lives within every human being and not only in temples.
  • The human body itself is a sacred temple.
  • True devotion is active, living and eternal, while material structures are temporary.
  • Simplicity, purity of heart and sincere faith are more important than rituals and expensive offerings.

Paragraph Answer

In this Vachana, Basavanna presents a beautiful and inclusive idea of spirituality. He explains that true devotion does not depend on wealth or magnificent temples. Although rich people may build grand temples, a poor person can worship God with equal sincerity because God resides within every human being. Basavanna compares the human body to a sacred temple, where the legs are the pillars, the body is the shrine and the head is the golden cupola. He emphasizes that stone temples may one day crumble, but sincere faith, devotion and good deeds remain forever. The Vachana teaches that spirituality is found in a pure heart, honest living and selfless devotion rather than in external rituals or expensive places of worship.
