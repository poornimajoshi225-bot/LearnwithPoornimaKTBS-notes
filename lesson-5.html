About the Lesson

What is Moral Action? is an inspiring lesson that explains the true meaning of morality. It teaches students that moral actions are performed without selfishness or expectation of reward. True morality comes from kindness, honesty, compassion, and helping others because it is the right thing to do.

II. COMPREHENSION QUESTIONS

A. Answer the following questions briefly:

1. In paragraph one, Gandhiji says that our conventional behaviour is:

Answer: (c) neither moral nor immoral.

2. What is non-moral action?

Answer: A non-moral action is an action that does not involve moral choice or intention. It is done out of habit, custom or routine.

3. What is the difference between a mechanical act and an intentional act?

Answer: A mechanical act is done automatically without thought, whereas an intentional act is performed consciously using one's own intelligence and free will.

4. Give an example each for conventional behaviour and a moral act.

Answer:

  • Conventional behaviour: Standing in a queue because everyone else is doing so.
  • Moral act: Helping a poor person without expecting any reward.

5. What is the important criterion mentioned in paragraph two?

Answer: A moral action must arise from one's own free will, intelligence and conscience.

6. When can the messenger's act become a moral action?

Answer: When he delivers the message because he believes it is his moral duty and not merely because he was ordered to do so.

7. When can we regard ourselves to have stepped on to the moral road?

Answer: When we act according to our conscience and not because of fear, reward or pressure.

8. What is the belief that Gandhi talks of in paragraph two?

Answer: The belief that God within us is the constant witness to all our actions.

9. When does a good action become a moral act? Give an example.

Answer: A good action becomes a moral act when it is done with a pure and selfless intention.

Example: Feeding a hungry person out of genuine compassion.

10. In the example of feeding the poor, whose action is moral?

Answer: The person who feeds the poor with genuine pity and kindness performs a moral action.

11. The result of our action determines whether our action is moral or non-moral.

Answer: False. Morality depends on intention, not on the result.

12. Why does Gandhi say Alexander's conquests cannot be called moral actions?

Answer: Alexander fought for conquest and fame, not for the welfare of humanity.

13. What criterion is added in paragraph four?

Answer: A moral act should be performed without fear or compulsion.

14. When does simple living become moral?

Answer: When a wealthy person voluntarily lives simply to help others and fulfil moral duty.

15. When does an employer's action of paying higher salaries remain non-moral?

Answer: When it is done only to gain profit, avoid strikes or prevent workers from leaving.

16. What argument does Gandhi strengthen by quoting Shakespeare?

Answer: That actions done for selfish gain or personal profit are not moral actions.

17. Henry Clay is mentioned as an example of:

Answer: Non-moral action.

18. What qualities of Daniel Webster does Gandhi mention?

Answer: His great intellect, noble character and heroic qualities.

19. What is the single mean act of Webster mentioned in paragraph six?

Answer: He sold his intellectual integrity for money.

20. Why is it difficult to judge the morality of a man's action?

Answer: It is difficult because we cannot know a person's true motives or intentions. Morality depends on the intention behind the action, not merely on the visible result.

21. What arguments does Gandhiji give to justify that a moral act should be free from fear and compulsion?

Answer: Gandhiji says that an action performed out of fear, pressure or compulsion has no moral value. A truly moral act must be performed freely because a person believes it is the right thing to do, not because of fear of punishment or hope of reward.

22. To sum up, an action becomes moral when an action:

Paragraph Criterion for a Moral Action
(a) Paragraph 2 Springs from one's own will.
(b) Paragraph 3 Is done with the intention to do good.
(c) Paragraph 4 Is done without fear or compulsion.
(d) Paragraph 5 Is free from selfishness or material gain.
(e) Paragraph 6 Is not done with the expectation of reward in the life after death.

B. Close Study

Read the following extracts carefully. Discuss in pairs and then write the answers to the questions given below them.

Extract 1

“The great Saint Theresa wished to have a torch in her right hand and a vessel of water in her left so that with the one she might burn the glories of heaven and with the other extinguish the fires of hell, and men might learn to serve God from love alone without fear of hell and without temptation of heavenly bliss.”

a. Why did Saint Theresa hold a torch in her right hand?

Answer: She wished to burn away the glories of heaven so that people would not serve God for the reward of heaven.

b. What does “a vessel of water” signify?

Answer: It signifies extinguishing the fires of hell, removing the fear of punishment from people's minds.

c. What was her message to humanity?

Answer: Her message was that people should serve God out of pure love, without expecting heavenly rewards or fearing hell.

Extract 2

“Wherever he went (in the course of his conquests), he took the Greek language and Greek culture, arts and manners, and today we enjoy the benefits of Greek civilization... It was all right that he was termed ‘great’, but moral he cannot be called.”

a. Who does 'he' refer to?

Answer: 'He' refers to Alexander the Great.

b. What is his contribution to mankind?

Answer: He spread the Greek language, culture, arts and manners, allowing future generations to enjoy the benefits of Greek civilization.

c. Why doesn't Gandhi consider him moral?

Answer: Gandhi believes Alexander's actions were driven by conquest, fame and personal ambition, not by a desire to do good. Therefore, they cannot be considered moral actions.

III. PARAGRAPH WRITING

Discuss in pairs/groups of four each the answers to the following questions. Individually, note down the important points for each question and then develop the points into one-paragraph answers.

1. Write on Mahatma Gandhi's comments on action prompted by the motive of happiness in another world.

Important Points

  • Actions performed for rewards in the afterlife are not moral.
  • True morality is completely selfless.
  • Good deeds should not be done to gain heaven or avoid hell.
  • Saint Francis Xavier and Saint Theresa rejected such selfish motives.
  • People should serve God out of love alone.

Paragraph Answer

Mahatma Gandhi believes that actions performed only to gain happiness in the next world cannot be called truly moral. According to him, if a person does good deeds merely to obtain heaven or escape the punishment of hell, the action is motivated by selfishness rather than genuine goodness. True morality lies in serving God and helping others with a pure heart, without expecting any reward. Gandhi refers to Saint Francis Xavier and Saint Theresa, who believed that people should love and serve God only out of devotion and not for personal gain. Therefore, moral actions should always be selfless, sincere and performed with love.

2. Why does Gandhi say that a moral act should be done without compulsion?

Important Points

  • Morality depends upon freedom of choice.
  • An act done under fear or pressure has no moral value.
  • Compulsion destroys independent thinking.
  • True moral actions arise from one's own conscience.
  • A moral act is performed because it is right, not because it is forced.

Paragraph Answer

Gandhi states that a moral act should always be performed without fear or compulsion. If a person acts only because of punishment, pressure or fear of losing something, the action loses its moral value. Such an action becomes mechanical rather than voluntary. True morality exists only when a person freely chooses to do what is right by listening to his or her conscience. Therefore, Gandhi believes that a moral action must arise from one's own free will and sense of duty rather than from external force or selfish motives.

IV. VOCABULARY EXERCISES

A. Discuss in pairs the similarity or difference in meaning and usage between the following pairs of words (Use a dictionary).

No. Word Pair Difference in Meaning / Usage
i Say / Tell Say focuses on the words spoken. Tell focuses on the person receiving the information.
ii Moral / Morale Moral relates to right and wrong. Morale means confidence or enthusiasm.
iii Custom / Habit Custom is a traditional social practice. Habit is a repeated personal behaviour.
iv Between / Among Between is used for two persons or things. Among is used for three or more.
v Enough / Sufficient Both mean adequate. Sufficient is more formal than enough.
vi Same / Similar Same means identical. Similar means nearly alike.
vii Culture / Civilisation Culture includes customs, arts and beliefs. Civilisation refers to the overall development of society.
viii Rise / Raise Rise happens by itself. Raise means lift something.
ix Sympathy / Empathy Sympathy means feeling sorry for someone. Empathy means understanding and sharing another's feelings.
x Practice / Practise In British English, practice is a noun and practise is a verb. In American English, practice is used for both.
xi Pardon / Forgive / Excuse Pardon is formal, forgive means letting go of anger, and excuse means overlooking a minor fault.
xii Mistake / Error / Blunder Mistake is general, error is a formal inaccuracy, and blunder is a careless or serious mistake.
xiii Conscience / Consciousness / Conscientious Conscience is the inner sense of right and wrong. Consciousness means awareness. Conscientious means careful and responsible.

V. LANGUAGE ACTIVITIES

A. Paragraph Writing

A coherent paragraph consists of a Topic Sentence (TS), Supporting Sentences (SS), and a Concluding Sentence (CS). Arrange the following jumbled sentences in a logical sequence and develop them into a coherent paragraph.

Logical Sequence

  1. (c) Mother Teresa – known for her compassion and humanity – born in 1910. (TS)
  2. (a) She founded the Missionaries of Charity in 1950. (SS)
  3. (d) Worked in the slums of Calcutta. (SS)
  4. (b) Received the Nobel Peace Prize in 1979. (SS)
  5. (e) Awarded the Bharat Ratna in 1980. (SS)
  6. (h) Passed away on 05 September 1997. (SS)
  7. (g) Received the title of "Blessed" through Beatification in 2003. (SS)
  8. (f) Rendered yeoman service to humankind. (CS)

Developed Paragraph

Mother Teresa was globally known for her compassion and humanity. She was born in 1910 and devoted her entire life to serving the poor and the sick. In 1950, she founded the Missionaries of Charity and worked tirelessly in the slums of Calcutta to help the needy. Her selfless service earned her the Nobel Peace Prize in 1979 and the Bharat Ratna in 1980. She passed away on 05 September 1997, but her legacy of love and service continued to inspire the world. In 2003, she was honoured with the title of "Blessed" through beatification. Mother Teresa rendered yeoman service to humankind and remains a symbol of compassion and selfless sacrifice.

C. Supply the Correct Past Tense

Read the following passage carefully and observe the correct use of the Past Tense and the Past Perfect Tense.

Answer

When the old lady returned to her flat, she saw at once that burglars had broken in during her absence because the front door was open and everything in the flat was upside down. The burglars themselves were no longer there, but they had probably just left because a cigarette was still burning on an ornamental table. Probably they had heard the lift coming up and had run down the fire escape. They had helped themselves to her whisky too, but there was a little left, so she poured herself a drink. She wondered if they had found her jewellery and rather hoped that they had. The jewellery had been given to her by her husband, who had died some years before. Since his death she had not had the heart to wear it, yet she had not liked to sell it. Now it seemed that fate had taken the matter out of her hands, and certainly the insurance money would come in handy.

Grammar Tip

  • Simple Past is used for completed actions in the past.
  • Past Perfect is used for an action that happened before another past action.
  • Past Perfect Passive is used when the subject receives the action (e.g., had been given).

IX. GRAMMAR REVISITED

D. Future Time

The word "Tense" refers to the form of the verb and not to time. English has only two tenses—Present and Past—but there are many ways to express future time. Study the following sentences and observe the difference in meaning.

Sentence Meaning / Usage
My father will meet the H.M. tomorrow. Future Simple
Expresses a future fact, prediction or simple future action.
My father is meeting the H.M. tomorrow. Present Continuous
Indicates a fixed arrangement or appointment already planned.
My father is going to meet the H.M. tomorrow. Be Going To
Shows a prior intention or plan decided before speaking.
My father will be meeting the H.M. tomorrow. Future Continuous
Indicates an action that will be in progress at a future time.
My father meets the H.M. tomorrow. Present Simple
Used for official schedules, timetables and fixed programmes.

Key Points

  • Will + Verb → Future fact, prediction or decision.
  • Present Continuous → Fixed future arrangement.
  • Be Going To → Future intention or plan.
  • Future Continuous → Action in progress in the future.
  • Present Simple → Official schedules and timetables.

No. Humour Explanation
1 Customer: "Waiter! There's a hair in my soup!"
Waiter: "That's alright, sir. It's hare soup." 		Hair and Hare sound alike. The waiter pretends the hair is part of rabbit (hare) soup.
2 "What do you call this?"

3 Teacher: Where is the English Channel?
John: We don't get it on our TV. 		John mistakes the English Channel (sea) for a television channel.
4 Priest: What is life?
Drink Addict: It depends on the liver. 		Liver means both the organ in the body and a person who lives.
5 Teacher: Where was King Solomon's temple?
Student: On his head. 		Temple means both a place of worship and the side of the head.

Because he was a skipper. 		Skipper means captain, while "skip" reminds us of skipping rope.

Another Pun Joke

Question: Why can't your nose be 12 inches long?

Answer: Because then it would be a foot!

Explanation: Foot refers both to a body part and to a unit of length (12 inches).
