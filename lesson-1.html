A. Comprehension Questions

1. "The man indulged in mad whims." Who considers the man's work as "mad whims?"

Answer: b) The writer.

In the context of the story, the narrator describes his actions as "mad whims" from the perspective of those around him, who laughed at his unusual work.

2. Explain the comparison in paragraph 3.

a) "Some boys" are compared to the man who did work that others considered useless.

b) "They are not studying" is compared to the man not doing any useful work.

c) "Yet passing in the test" is compared to the man being allowed into Heaven despite his "useless" life on Earth.

3. When the men say, "We haven't a moment to spare" (Para 6), it means that

Answer: b) They are happy and proud to be so busy.

The text explains that saying these words makes them feel happy and exalted.

4. Why is the torrent in the Workers' Paradise silent?

Answer: Because, in the Workers' Paradise, even nature is expected to be productive. Therefore, the torrent does not "waste its energy" by singing.

5. "The girl's hair was carelessly done." (Para 9) This indicates that

Answer: a) She was interested only in her work.

She was completely occupied with her duties and gave little attention to her personal appearance.

6. Why did the "girl of the silent torrent" feel sorry for the man?

Answer: She felt sorry for him because he had no work to do. In her world, a person without work was considered unfortunate and aimless.

7. "The girl scanned the painted pot at home secretly." (Para 27) This line indicates that

Answer: b) The girl did not want the artist to know that she was impressed by his work.

Although she admired the beauty of the painted pot, she hid her feelings because she belonged to a society that valued only useful work.

8. "The hurrying feet" of the girl became "less hurried" because

Answer: d) The meaningless was slowly becoming meaningful.

The artist's work introduced her to beauty beyond utility, and her understanding of time and life gradually changed.

9. Why did the elders of the Workers' Paradise become anxious?

Answer: They became anxious because the efficiency of their paradise began to decline. The people, inspired by the artist, started spending time on painting, sculpture and other creative activities instead of working continuously.

10. The girl follows the man out of the Workers' Paradise. This suggests that the girl

Answer: c) Saw new possibilities open up before her.

She realized that life is not only about work but also about beauty, imagination and personal happiness.

11. What does the line "the man never believed in mere utility" mean?

Answer: It means that the man believed life should not be measured only by usefulness. He valued creativity, beauty, imagination and joy, which make life meaningful even if they have no practical purpose.

12. What changes occur in the girl's behaviour/attitude at the following stages?

a) Before: She was completely devoted to work and efficiency. She pitied anyone who had no work.

b) After seeing the painted pitcher: She became curious and thoughtful. The beauty of the artwork remained in her mind and made her think differently.

c) When she wore the ribbons: She willingly spent time on beautiful things and realized that not everything in life needs to be useful.

d) When she left the Workers' Paradise: She chose freedom, beauty and creativity over a life devoted only to work.

13. At the end of the story, there is a complete change in the girl's attitude to life. Who should the credit go to?

Answer: The credit goes to both the artist and the girl. The artist inspired her by showing the beauty of art and imagination, while the girl's curiosity and willingness to accept new ideas made her transformation possible. Without her open mind, the artist's influence would not have changed her life.

B. Close Study

1. "But the Moving Finger writes even in heaven."

a) What does "Moving Finger" mean here?

Answer: It refers to fate or destiny. It implies that even in a place as structured as Heaven, events are guided by an inevitable divine plan that cannot be changed.

b) What figure of speech is used in "Moving Finger"?

Answer: The figure of speech is Metonymy (Personification). The "Moving Finger" represents fate or destiny that records the events of life.

c) What does the sentence mean in the context?

Answer: It means that even in the perfect Workers' Paradise, unexpected events are destined to happen. The arrival of the artist was part of fate and could not be prevented.

2. "As a princess sees a lonely beggar and is filled with pity, so the busy girl of heaven was filled with pity."

a) Who is compared to a lonely beggar?

Answer: The artist (the man who had no work) is compared to a lonely beggar.

b) Why was the girl filled with pity?

Answer: She saw him standing near the stream with no work to do. In the Workers' Paradise, a person without work was considered unfortunate, so she felt sorry for him.

c) How did the girl offer to help him?

Answer: She kindly offered to share some of her work with him by saying, "I shall spare some work for you to do, if you like."

3. "When she set out for the torrent the next day, her hurrying feet were a little less hurried than before."

a) What had happened the previous night?

Answer: She had taken home the painted pitcher made by the artist. She secretly admired its beautiful designs and kept looking at it with curiosity.

b) What does "hurrying feet" suggest?

Answer: It symbolizes her busy life, constant work, efficiency and devotion to productivity in the Workers' Paradise.

c) Why did they become less hurried?

Answer: The artist's work changed her way of thinking. She began to appreciate beauty, creativity and the joy of living, instead of thinking only about work.

III. Paragraph Writing

1. Contrast in Attitudes: The Workers vs. The Artist

Answer:

The attitude toward time and life represents a fundamental difference between the inhabitants of the Workers' Paradise and the artist. The workers believe that time should always be used productively. They consider leisure a waste and take pride in being constantly busy. Their statement, "We haven't a moment to spare," shows that they value efficiency above everything else.

In contrast, the artist believes that life is not meant only for work. He values imagination, creativity and beauty. When he says he has "no moment to spare for work," he is not being lazy; instead, he believes that art and self-expression are equally important. The workers measure success through productivity, while the artist measures it through creativity and happiness.

The story highlights the conflict between a life devoted only to work and a life enriched by beauty, imagination and freedom.

2. The Two Worlds: Which is Better?

Answer:

The story presents two different worlds. The Workers' Paradise is a place where everything is based on usefulness and efficiency. People work continuously and have no time to enjoy beauty or creativity. Although this world is well organized, it lacks happiness and emotional richness.

The artist's world is filled with imagination, beauty and freedom. It teaches people to appreciate art, nature and the simple joys of life. Through the artist, the girl discovers that life is more meaningful when it includes creativity along with work.

Therefore, the artist's world is better because it balances work with beauty and allows people to experience true happiness and personal growth.

3. Debate Points: Aesthetics vs. Utility

Aesthetics (Art / Knowledge for its Own Sake) Utility (Art / Knowledge for a Specific Purpose)
Art satisfies the human spirit and brings happiness. Practical work ensures the survival and development of society.
Creativity encourages innovation and fresh ideas. Efficiency helps achieve goals quickly without wasting resources.
Beauty and imagination make life meaningful. Useful skills provide employment and economic stability.
Knowledge gained for curiosity often leads to great discoveries. Applied knowledge solves practical problems and improves daily life.
Art develops emotions, culture and personal expression. Utility focuses on productivity, discipline and progress.

C. Sub-skills of Reading

The purpose of reading a passage or text determines the way we read it. We look only at the main points when we want general information. This is called Skimming. When we read carefully to find specific information, it is called Scanning.

i) Skimming

News Item

A Korean Airbus with 199 passengers crashed 5 km away from Tripoli Airport in Libya and burst into flames, killing at least 100 people. The official Libyan News Agency reported that the plane fell on two houses, killing four persons in one of them. About 80–100 people, including the captain, survived the crash. The accident occurred at 7:00 a.m., about 25 minutes before the expected landing time.

Choose the most appropriate heading:

  1. Hundred feared killed in plane crash
  2. Plane crashes before landing
  3. Tragedy near Tripoli
  4. Captain survives plane crash
  5. Plane falls on houses
  6. Airbus in flames

Answer: 1. Hundred feared killed in plane crash.

Justification:

A good newspaper headline should highlight the most important event. The major focus of this report is the large number of deaths in the plane crash. Therefore, "Hundred feared killed in plane crash" is the most suitable heading.

VI. Speaking Activities

A. Role Play

Look at the conversation in the story between the girl and the man from:

  • Paragraphs 11–19
  • Paragraphs 20–26
  • Paragraphs 30–35

Work in pairs. One student should take the role of the Man and the other the role of the Girl. Read the conversation aloud.

Role Play Script

1. Paragraphs 11–19 (The Initial Encounter)

Girl: "A-ha! You have no work in hand, have you?"

Man: "Work! I've not a moment to spare for work."

Girl: "I shall spare some work for you to do, if you like."

Man: "Girl of the silent torrent, all this time I have been waiting to take some work from your hands."

Girl: "What kind of work would you like?"

Man: "Will you give me one of your pitchers, one that you can spare?"

Girl: "A pitcher? You want to draw water from the torrent?"

Man: "No, I shall draw pictures on your pitcher."

Girl: "Pictures, indeed! I have no time to waste on such as you. I'm going."

2. Paragraphs 20–26 (The Persistence)

Man: "Girl of the silent torrent, give me one of your clay pitchers. I shall draw pictures on it!"

Girl: "What do they mean, all those lines and colours? What is their purpose?"

Man: "Nothing. A picture may have no meaning and serve no purpose."

3. Paragraphs 30–35 (The Artistic Influence)

Girl: "What do you want of me?"

Man: "Only some more work from your hands."

Girl: "What kind of work would you like?"

Man: "Let me make a coloured ribbon for your hair."

Girl: "And what for?"

Man: "Nothing."

B. Making Requests

The sentence "Will you give me one of your pitchers?" is a polite request.

Other polite ways of making requests are:

  • Would you give me __________?
  • Could you give me __________?
  • Will you please give me __________?
  • Would you mind if I use your __________?

Situation

Your car breaks down in the middle of a busy road. You stop a biker and his friend and request them to help you push the car to the side of the road.

Sample Requests

1. Polite Request

"Excuse me, I'm terribly sorry to bother you, but would you mind helping me push my car to the side of the road? It has broken down."

2. Direct Request

"Hi! My car has broken down in the middle of the road. Could you please help me push it to the side?"

3. Using 'Would you mind'

"Excuse me, I'm in a bit of trouble. Would you mind giving me a hand to push my car off the road? I'd really appreciate your help."
